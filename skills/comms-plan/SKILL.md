# Communications Plan

Design the communication strategy for a product story. Maps every relevant audience, adapts the story for each, selects the right format and channel, plans the pre-sell sequence, and prepares the PM for both proactive and reactive storytelling situations.

## When to Use

- **After building a story**: The story structure and content exist — now the PM needs a plan to land it
- **Before a big pitch**: Need to orchestrate the sequence of conversations that leads to a successful leadership decision
- **Cross-functional alignment**: Need to get multiple teams on board with different motivations
- **Standalone**: The PM already knows their story but needs help with the communication strategy

## Core Insight: Communication > Content

How a PM communicates their story is a stronger predictor of success than the content of the story itself. Good communication can overcome story shortcomings. Bad communication can kill a great story. The most common reason PMs fail to get buy-in is not that their idea was wrong — it's that they treated the pitch as a single event instead of an orchestrated sequence.

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Context

Ask the PM for:
- **The story**: What initiative are they communicating? (If a story-builder document exists in workspace, offer to pull from it.)
- **The goal**: What outcome does the PM need? (Approval, resources, alignment, dependency unlocked, team motivation)
- **The timeline**: When does this need to land? Is there a specific meeting or review date?
- **Current state**: Has anyone already heard this story? What's the starting point?
- **Output format**: What format for the final document? (docx, md, or pdf). Default: docx.

### Step 2: Map All Audiences

Help the PM identify every audience that matters. For each audience, capture:

**Audience identification**:
- Who specifically? (Names and roles, not just "leadership")
- What do you need from them? (Be precise: approval, headcount, prioritization, execution, advocacy)

**Audience understanding** — for each person or group, assess five dimensions:

1. **Context**: What's their current situation? Under pressure? Exceeding goals? Going through reorg? Stressed or confident?

2. **Goals**: What are they personally trying to achieve? What metrics are they measured on? What do they care about most?

3. **Relationship to the story**: Where are they right now?
   - *Involved*: They have context, know the current state, have been part of decisions
   - *Informed*: They know the team exists and roughly what it's doing
   - *Invisible*: They don't know about this initiative at all

4. **Relationship to PM and team**: Trust level? Working relationship history? Any unresolved tensions?

5. **Communication style**: Do they prefer details or summaries? Written docs or live presentations? Pre-reads or live discussion? Data-heavy or narrative-heavy?

**Three audience categories**:

**Leadership audience** — People who control approval, resources, and strategic direction.
- Key question: Who are the gatekeepers? (The most opinionated leaders who most directly control the approval and resourcing of this initiative.)
- If PM isn't sure, suggest: look at the org chart, start with your direct management chain plus leaders of any dependent teams.

**Team audience** — People who will execute the work.
- Key question: Do you need alignment only, or do you also need motivation and inspiration?
- Goal is not just getting the team to do the work, but getting them to *own* the mission. The difference between a team that executes a spec and a team that brings creative energy and urgency to a problem is the difference between mercenaries and missionaries.

**Dependent stakeholder audience** — People outside your direct team whose cooperation you need.
- Key question: What exactly do you need from each stakeholder, and how does your story connect to their goals?
- These are the hardest audiences because you have the least natural influence over them.

### Step 3: Adapt the Story for Each Audience

The core story stays the same. What changes is emphasis, detail level, and framing.

**For leadership**:

What they need to hear:
- *It's aligned*: Connect explicitly to company strategy and leadership's stated goals. Don't assume they'll make the connection — spell it out.
- *The customer problem is real*: Show evidence that this is a real, significant problem worth solving. Use data and research, not opinions.
- *It's valuable to the business*: Size the opportunity. What's the ROI? What's the expected impact on the metrics they track?
- *It's achievable*: How much will it cost? How long until results? What resources are needed?

Adapt the ask: Make it specific to what you need from leadership — approval, budget amount, headcount, strategic priority decision.

Communication style: Lead with an executive summary (elevator pitch), have a pre-read with details available, focus live discussion time on the ask and their questions. Don't bury the ask at the end of a 30-minute presentation.

**For teams**:

What they need to hear:
- *Why this matters*: Connect to company goals briefly, but spend more time on *why this is the right mission for this team*
- *What changes*: Be specific about what gets deprioritized and what's new. Give the team time to react.
- *The customer's reality*: Share the emotional, human side of the story. Customer quotes, journey maps, real user pain. This is what turns a roadmap item into a mission.
- *Their role*: Why is this team uniquely positioned to solve this problem?

Adapt the ask: What you need from the team — not just execution, but ideation, creative problem-solving, ownership.

Communication style: Teams usually want time to discuss, ask questions, and process. Workshops, team sessions, and collaborative formats work better than one-way presentations. Quick context on strategy, then deep dive on the problem and the plan.

**For dependent stakeholders**:

What they need to hear:
- *Goal alignment*: How does your story connect to *their* goals? Use their language, connect to their metrics.
- *The specific ask*: What exactly do you need from their team? Alignment, a milestone, a deliverable, collaboration on an idea?
- *The execution context*: How and why does the dependency matter? Show you've done your homework.
- *You've done your part*: Show what your team is delivering. Make it clear you're not just dumping work on them.

Adapt the ask: Be precise — "We need your team to deliver the API by March 15" not "We need engineering support."

Communication style: Varies widely. For their leadership, use pre-sell methods. For their teams, include them in planning workshops. For stakeholders who just need to stay informed, use async updates.

### Step 4: Plan the Pre-Sell Sequence

The pitch meeting should be the final step, not the first. When the PM gets to the formal pitch, every decision-maker in the room should already understand and support the story. The pitch is confirmation, not persuasion.

**Why pre-selling works**: When leaders encounter an idea for the first time in a group setting, they naturally pressure-test and ask tough questions. When multiple leaders do this simultaneously, they can undermine each other's confidence. Pre-selling lets each leader ask their questions privately, get answers, and arrive at the group meeting already aligned.

**Four pre-sell methods** (from most to least intensive):

**1. Co-creation**
- *What*: Work with the leader to build the story together
- *When to use*: The leader is highly important to the decision AND is currently against or neutral on the idea
- *How to prepare*: Come with ideas and 30-50% of the story at low fidelity. Leave room for them to shape it.
- *Why it works*: People naturally support ideas they feel ownership of

**2. Feedback**
- *What*: Share the story and ask for their input to make it stronger
- *When to use*: The leader is moderately important AND is neutral or mildly supportive
- *How to prepare*: Have 50-75% of the story ready. Don't wait until it's finished — you need room to incorporate their feedback.
- *Why it works*: Invests the leader in the outcome, especially when they see their feedback reflected in the final version

**3. Advice**
- *What*: Ask the leader for advice on how to best present this to the broader leadership team
- *When to use*: The leader is less central to the decision but still influential, OR you don't have a strong relationship yet
- *How to prepare*: Have 80%+ of the story at higher fidelity so they can fully grasp it
- *Why it works*: Gives context without demanding much from them. They become invested in your success because they want their advice to pay off.

**4. Pre-read**
- *What*: Send the complete story for async review before the pitch meeting
- *When to use*: The leader is supportive or unlikely to oppose, OR you can't get their time
- *How to prepare*: 95-100% complete and polished. This IS the final product.
- *Why it works*: Lowest barrier. At minimum, ensures no one is blindsided at the pitch meeting.

**Choosing the right method for each leader**:

Map each leader on two axes:
- **Importance to the decision**: How much do they control the outcome?
- **Current stance**: Supportive, neutral, or opposed?

| | Supportive | Neutral | Opposed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High importance** | Feedback or Advice | Co-creation or Feedback | Co-creation |
| **Medium importance** | Pre-read or Advice | Feedback or Advice | Feedback |
| **Low importance** | Pre-read | Pre-read | Advice |

### Step 5: Choose Format and Channel

**Formats** — how the story is packaged:

| Format | Best for | Strengths | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Presentation (slides) | Leadership pitches, team kickoffs | Visual, structured, shareable | Can feel one-directional |
| Written document (one-pager, memo) | Pre-reads, async alignment, complex stories | Detailed, reviewable, forces clarity | Some audiences won't read it |
| Video | Async storytelling, broad audiences | Emotional, personal, scalable | High production effort, hard to iterate |
| Conversation (no docs) | 1:1 pre-sells, early stage ideas | Casual, flexible, builds relationship | No artifact for others to reference |
| Design/diagram (whiteboard, Miro) | Workshops, team exploration | Collaborative, visual | Can feel unfinished to leadership |

**Channels** — where and how the story is delivered:

| Channel | Best for | Strengths | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Large meeting | Final pitch, broad alignment | Everyone hears the same thing | Hard to have real discussion |
| Small meeting | Pre-sells, focused decisions | Real discussion, relationship building | Limited reach |
| 1:1 | Pre-sells with key leaders | Deep, personal, trust-building | Time-intensive |
| Email / Slack | Informing, pre-reads, updates | Async, low-friction | Easy to ignore, no discussion |
| Workshop | Team alignment, co-creation | Collaborative, deep engagement | Time-intensive, needs facilitation |

**Selection guide**: Match format and channel to the audience and the phase.

For **leadership pitch**: Pre-read (written) → small meeting pre-sells (1:1 or small group) → final pitch (large meeting with slides)

For **team alignment**: Presentation or workshop for kickoff → ongoing updates via Slack/email → workshops for planning

For **dependent stakeholders**: 1:1 or small meetings for key dependencies → email/Slack for informed stakeholders → include in planning workshops if they have execution responsibilities

Follow company culture. If the company is meeting-heavy, don't fight it — work within it. If the company prefers written docs, lead with a memo.

### Step 6: Build the Communication Sequence

Help the PM create a timeline that sequences all pre-sells, team communications, and stakeholder outreach leading up to the decision point.

The sequence should:
- Start with the people whose buy-in you need most (or who are hardest to convince)
- Leave enough time between pre-sell conversations and the final pitch for the PM to incorporate feedback
- Ensure no one important is blindsided at the final meeting
- Include a plan for communicating the outcome (whether it's approved or not)

### Step 7: Prepare for Reactive Storytelling

Not all storytelling is planned. The PM will get asked about this initiative in hallways, Slack threads, and meetings they didn't expect. Help them prepare:

**The elevator pitch**: 30-second version that covers character, problem, result, action, ask. Should feel natural, not rehearsed. (Pull from story-builder document if available.)

**3-5 talking points**: The key messages that the PM always comes back to, regardless of the question. These are the anchors.

**Pre-built Q&A**: The 5-7 toughest questions someone might ask, with prepared answers:
- How does this connect to company goals?
- How did you validate the opportunity?
- What other options did you consider?
- Why now?
- What does this displace?
- What's the risk if we don't do this?
- What's the cost?

**Reactive techniques**:
- If you know the answer: speak up, hit your talking points, answer the question directly
- If you don't know the answer: acknowledge the question, bridge back to your talking points, commit to following up — and actually follow up
- If you're surprised-pitched (asked to present unexpectedly): use the elevator pitch, offer to follow up with the full story and documentation

## Proactive Storytelling Modes

Different phases of the product lifecycle call for different communication modes. Help the PM pick the right one:

**Pitch mode**: Building the case for a new initiative. Use the full story structure + pre-sell sequence. Best for: discovery/planning phase, seeking approval.

**Inform mode**: Keeping stakeholders updated during execution. No ask — just visibility. Use async channels (email, Slack, dashboards). Best for: execution phase.

**Report mode**: Sharing results after delivery. Focus on what was achieved vs. what was promised. Builds trust for the next story. Best for: post-launch.

**Workshop mode**: Collaboratively building or refining the story with an audience. Best for: team alignment, low-trust situations, and early-stage ideas that need collective input.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating the pitch as a one-shot event**: Thinking of it as one big presentation where you either win or lose. In reality, the pitch should be the final formality in a pre-sold decision. If you walk into the pitch room without knowing the outcome, you've already lost.

2. **Same story for every audience**: Leadership cares about strategic alignment and ROI. Teams care about mission and execution clarity. Stakeholders care about how it affects their goals. Adapt.

3. **Skipping the pre-sell**: The single most common communication failure. Pre-selling takes time and effort but has a dramatically higher success rate than cold-pitching.

4. **Not following up**: Promising to "get back to you on that" and then not doing it destroys trust faster than anything else. Track every commitment and deliver on it.

5. **Over-investing in format, under-investing in sequence**: A beautiful slide deck doesn't compensate for blindsiding a key leader. The sequence of conversations matters more than the polish of any single artifact.

6. **Ignoring company culture**: If the company communicates through written memos, don't insist on a live presentation. If they communicate through meetings, don't send a Slack essay. Work within the culture.

7. **Forgetting to communicate after the decision**: Whether the story gets approved or not, the team and stakeholders need to hear the outcome. Silence after a decision erodes trust.

8. **Not reading the room**: Pushing a big investment pitch when the company is cutting costs. Proposing a casual workshop when leadership expects a formal presentation. Match the energy of the room.

## Key Principle

Buy-in is not an event — it's a sequence. The PM who walks into a leadership meeting with every decision-maker already aligned has a 90% chance of success. The PM who walks in cold has a 10% chance. The communications plan is what turns 10% into 90%.
